# Braided emotion process boundary

## Status

Candidate-only formal carrier. This surface is not a clinical theory, diagnostic ontology, therapeutic protocol, or claim that one disputed emotion theory has won.

## Core claim

An emotion episode is represented as a temporally extended mode of organism-world coordination. The formal carrier keeps the following strands coupled but non-identical:

- bodily regulation;
- interoception;
- predictive interpretation;
- appraisal and significance;
- action readiness;
- concept and language;
- social and relational situation;
- conscious experience.

The compact statement is:

> Emotion is a constrained construction enacted by embodied organisms in consequential relationships; labels and theories remain useful, revisable, scale-indexed, and non-authoritative.

## Why this is a braid rather than a fusion

The implementation does not average competing theories into a single vague account. It records each theory family with a strongest question, a preserved insight, and a known blind spot. Local adequacy is allowed; global reduction is not required. Disagreement remains visible.

The knowledge braid likewise keeps provenance, method, warrant, purpose, and obligation explicit across neural-experimental, phenomenological, relational, cultural-narrative, and ethical routes. These routes may constrain one another, but none is marked replaceable by another route. This blocks extraction in which a relational or culturally situated account is stripped of provenance and retained only after translation into a neuroscientific variable.

## Labels

An emotion label is not identified with the episode. A label is represented as provisional and revisable, with all four possibilities retained:

- it can disclose structure;
- it can compress structure;
- it can reshape the ongoing episode;
- it can obscure the episode.

The practical boundary is therefore neither label-first nor label-free. The canonical observation sequence is:

1. low-inference sensation;
2. action tendency;
3. appraisal and context;
4. candidate label;
5. relational obligation;
6. chosen action.

Action may proceed without perfect classification, and no clinical protocol is claimed.

## Abstract

Historical physics-unification programs often fail at the boundary between
mathematical ambition and claim control. Candidate carriers, limiting
procedures, empirical comparisons, and physical recovery claims are introduced
in the same prose layer, while the distinction between theorem, diagnostic,
calibration target, external authority, and unresolved obstruction remains
informal. DASHI addresses that failure mode by staging physics unification as
a constructive typed program: carrier geometry, filtrations, projection-defect
structure, empirical-contact receipts, and obstruction surfaces are represented
inside a shared fail-closed promotion architecture.

This paper presents the current Paper 1 closure frontier of that program. The
thesis is physics unification, not governance tooling. Typed claim governance
is the enforcement architecture that prevents request surfaces, diagnostics,
or future-work routes from being promoted into completed physics. A positive
claim is admissible only when it is tied to an inhabited theorem, receipt, or
audit surface at the relevant canonical definition site. Missing constructors,
constructorless authority tokens, blocked promotion routes, and request-only
modules are therefore not hidden TODOs; they are first-class boundary objects
that determine the manuscript's admissible physics claims.

The current positive inventory is deliberately bounded. DASHI contains
direction-indexed G2 SFGC schema adoption for current core-facing consumers,
an official tracked G6 cross-lane commuting route with above-threshold
coordinate independence, constructive finite-support G3 selected-operator
subtraction support, an E8/LILA integer and local semantic boundary with
upstream promotion explicitly blocked, and an inhabited bounded W3 below-Z
Drell-Yan `t43` comparison-law receipt with `chi2/dof = 2.1565191176` under a
local threshold `4.0`. The stricter `chi2/dof <= 2` protocol and the later
strict log-covariance diagnostic do not pass; those failures are preserved as
negative diagnostics and shape-law obstructions rather than erased by the
bounded receipt.

DASHI is therefore presented here as a constructive mechanized
physics-unification architecture with an explicit closure frontier. The paper
does not claim completed unification, accepted external empirical authority,
physical calibration, sourced non-flat GR, Schwarzschild recovery, continuum
recovery, GRQFT closure, limited SM+GR closure, upstream E8 completion, or a
completed experimentally closed theory of everything. Its contribution is the
integration of carrier geometry, typed admissibility, mechanized closure
surfaces, bounded empirical contact, and exact obstruction accounting into one
physics-unification program.

## Introduction

Physics unification is not only a problem of finding the right equations. It
is also a problem of preserving the status of every intermediate claim. A
candidate unification program may contain useful carriers, suggestive
operator structures, empirical diagnostics, limiting procedures, and
mathematical analogies, but those objects do not have the same epistemic
status. A theorem is not a calibration. A diagnostic is not validation. A
request for an external authority token is not the authority token. A local
semantic construction is not an upstream mathematical completion. When these
distinctions are handled only in prose, a unification program can silently
convert aspiration into conclusion.

DASHI is organized around the opposite discipline. It treats unification as a
constructive closure problem over typed surfaces. The program's mathematical
and physics-facing objects are not allowed to promote themselves by rhetoric:
they must pass through explicit admissibility routes. A theorem, receipt, or
audit can promote only the claim authorized by its owning surface. A missing
constructor, blocked route, request-only module, or failed diagnostic is kept
as part of the formal frontier rather than being smoothed over in the
manuscript narrative.

The central claim of this paper is that DASHI provides a fail-closed
constructive physics-unification architecture. This should be read precisely.
The paper is not a governance-only paper whose main subject is process. The
subject is a physics-unification program: carrier geometry, prime/trit and
`FactorVec` coordinates, filtrations, lane actions, projection-defect
structure, finite-support operator surfaces, local E8/LILA structure,
empirical-contact receipts, and compression/refinement semantics are all part
of the unification substrate. The governance layer matters because it is the
mechanism that keeps this ambitious substrate publishable: it distinguishes
the inhabited physics-facing surfaces from the remaining obligations.

The resulting paper has a conventional narrative shell but a constructive
proof topology. It begins by explaining the mathematical ingredients needed
to read the program: ultrametric and p-adic structure, discrete gauge and
carrier schemas, contraction and filtration, E8 root-system boundaries, and
mechanized mathematics. It then introduces the DASHI architecture: carrier
coordinates, projection-defect decomposition, admissibility gates, receipts,
obstructions, and semantic compression surfaces. The technical sections stage
the currently inhabited local surfaces and the exact blockers that prevent
broader promotion.

Several positive surfaces are available today. The G2 lane supports
direction-indexed SFGC schema adoption for current core-facing consumers; it
is not Maxwell closure or a field-equation derivation. The G6 lane supports
the official tracked cross-lane commuting route and tracked above-threshold
coordinate independence; the older universal `LaneOperator` promotion route
remains blocked. The G3 lane supports certified subtraction for constructive
finite-support selected operators; associated-graded quotient closure, global
Schrodinger promotion, and arbitrary `WaveFunctionOperator` closure remain
blocked. The E8/LILA lane supports integer and local semantic structure while
preserving the upstream `E8RootEnumerationComplete` promotion boundary.

The empirical contact is also intentionally bounded. DASHI has an inhabited
W3 below-Z Drell-Yan `t43` comparison-law receipt, with `chi2/dof =
2.1565191176`, local threshold `4.0`, and mean prediction/data ratio
`0.9941233097`. This is a local positive receipt for the stated comparison
surface. It is not accepted external empirical authority, not above-Z
promotion, not W4/W5 convention closure, not physical calibration, and not
full empirical adequacy. The stricter `chi2/dof <= 2` protocol fails, and the
strict log-covariance diagnostic also fails. The paper uses those failures in
the strongest possible way: as evidence that the architecture blocks its own
over-promotion and records the next shape-law obstruction.

This is the sense in which DASHI is both a unification paper and a
fail-closed paper. The unification claim is constructive: DASHI stages a
single typed architecture in which carrier structure, operators, filtrations,
projection defects, empirical receipts, and obstruction surfaces can be
composed. The closure claim is bounded: only inhabited surfaces promote. The
remaining physics targets are not denied or hidden; they are named as exact
obligations, including accepted W3 authority, W4/W5 convention authority,
stress-energy construction, non-flat GR, continuum recovery, strict
Drell-Yan success, upstream E8 completion, and completed unification.

The paper's practical test is therefore not whether every downstream physics
claim is closed. It is whether the current carrier and closure architecture is
mathematically serious enough that the remaining gaps are concrete finite
obligations rather than rhetorical mysteries. Paper 1 argues for that
threshold: it presents DASHI as a constructive mechanized physics-unification
program with exact closure boundaries.

## What This Paper Does Not Claim

This paper does not claim that DASHI has completed physics unification. It
does not claim an accepted theory of everything, accepted external empirical
authority, physical calibration, sourced Einstein equations, non-flat GR
closure, Schwarzschild recovery, continuum recovery, GRQFT closure, limited
SM+GR closure, strict Drell-Yan success, above-Z Drell-Yan promotion, W4/W5
authority closure, upstream E8 completion, universal G6 closure, arbitrary
wavefunction-operator closure, chemistry closure, biology prediction,
perceptual validation, or cross-domain recovery equivalence. Any such phrase
belongs only in the manuscript as a blocked claim, request surface,
diagnostic, limitation, or future-work obligation.

## Conclusion

DASHI should be read as a constructive physics-unification program whose
central discipline is typed closure. The paper's thesis is not that the
program has already completed all physics. It is that unification can be
staged inside a typed architecture where carrier geometry, filtrations,
projection-defect structure, local theorem surfaces, empirical-contact
receipts, and obstruction objects are part of the same formal control
surface.

This distinction is essential. If the typed closure layer were removed, the
remaining manuscript would risk becoming another unification narrative whose
ambition outruns its proof status. With the closure layer in place, DASHI can
make a stronger and more precise claim: its current physics-unification route
has inhabited local surfaces and named remaining obligations. G2, tracked G6,
selected finite-support G3, E8/LILA boundary structure, and bounded W3
Drell-Yan contact can be stated positively only within their authorized
surfaces. The stronger empirical, GR, upstream E8, universal operator, and
completed-unification claims remain blocked.

The result is a paper that is ambitious without being closed by assertion. It
does not ask the reader to accept that all physics has been solved. It asks
the reader to evaluate a constructive mechanized unification architecture in
which closure, non-closure, empirical contact, and obstruction are represented
explicitly. That architecture is the contribution of Paper 1: a fail-closed
physics-unification program with a visible, typed frontier.

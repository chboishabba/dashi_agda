# 2. Three Generations from Heegner Discriminants

The matter carrier begins with the class-number-one imaginary quadratic
discriminants.  The quark sector uses the first three conductor lanes in this
list:

\[
D=-4,\qquad D=-3,\qquad D=-7.
\]

These are the discriminants of
\[
\mathbb{Q}(i),\qquad \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-3}),\qquad
\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-7}),
\]
respectively.  Their modular levels are
\[
N_{-4}=4,\qquad N_{-3}=3,\qquad N_{-7}=7.
\]
The three levels are pairwise coprime:
\[
\gcd(4,3)=1,\qquad \gcd(4,7)=1,\qquad \gcd(3,7)=1.
\]
This pairwise coprimality is the structural feature used to identify the
three quark lanes.  The lanes occupy non-overlapping conductor sectors of the
modular curve spectrum.

The class-number-one discriminants relevant here are
\[
-3,\ -4,\ -7,\ -8,\ -11,\ -19,\ -43,\ -67,\ -163.
\]
The assignment is positional.  The first three occupied conductor lanes form
the minimal quark triple
\[
\{3,4,7\}.
\]
Later class-number-one discriminants are not used to create additional quark
generations.  They are assigned to the lepton and beyond-Standard-Model
sectors by the exhaustion rule of the carrier.

It is important not to state the exhaustion more strongly than the arithmetic
allows.  There are later prime discriminants, such as \(-11\), \(-19\),
\(-43\), \(-67\), and \(-163\), whose prime levels are coprime to \(3\), \(4\),
and \(7\).  Thus the claim is not that no further coprime prime levels exist.
The claim is instead that the carrier fills the three quark slots with the
first three Heegner conductor lanes and then changes sector.  The fourth and
subsequent class-number-one discriminants are not promoted to fourth, fifth,
or later quark generations.

This gives a finite three-generation statement:

\[
\text{quark generations}
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\{-4,-3,-7\}
\quad\longleftrightarrow\quad
\{4,3,7\}.
\]

The statement is structural rather than phenomenological.  It does not use
the measured quark masses or CKM angles to choose the number three.  The
number of quark lanes comes from the ordered Heegner list together with the
pairwise conductor-coprimality criterion.  The following sections then ask how
the remaining Heegner positions are organised into lepton lanes and how the
same bookkeeping gives the \(48\)-fermion count.
